Issue 004  |  April 20, 2026

What happened this week in AI and health practice governance.

What it means for your practice.

What to do about it.

Signal 1

Twenty-one leading language models failed clinical reasoning more than 80% of the time when information was incomplete

A study published April 13 in *JAMA Network Open* by researchers at Mass General Brigham evaluated 21 large language models across standardized patient vignettes. All 21 failed to produce an appropriate differential more than 80% of the time when working from incomplete data. Final diagnosis accuracy only passed 90% when every pertinent detail was supplied up front. Lead researcher Dr. Marc Succi stated the models "are not ready for unsupervised clinical-grade deployment." The team introduced a new benchmark, PrIME-LLM, that scores the full reasoning arc rather than the final answer.

What this means for you

Signal 2

A federal bill would classify AI systems as products, opening the door to product liability claims

Senators Josh Hawley and Dick Durbin introduced the AI LEAD Act (Leading Ethical AI Development for Startups) in fall 2025. If enacted, it would classify AI systems as products under civil law, allowing consumers to bring product liability claims directly against developers and deployers that cause harm. Legal analysts expect the bill to reshape who carries exposure when an AI tool contributes to a bad clinical outcome, shifting some weight back toward vendors while leaving the deploying practitioner squarely on the hook for clinical judgment. The bill sits alongside a 2024 Louisiana ruling (Dickson v. Dexcom) that is being watched for whether FDA authorization blocks broader liability claims on AI-enabled devices.

What this means for you

Signal 3

The EU AI Act high-risk deadline is August 2. Every clinical decision support tool in your stack is in scope.

The EU AI Act becomes fully applicable on August 2, 2026. Diagnostic aid systems, clinical decision support tools, and patient monitoring systems are classified as high risk under Annex III. High-risk obligations include risk management, human oversight, data governance, transparency, conformity assessment, CE marking, and EU database registration. European Commission guidelines on the practical application of the high-risk classification are rolling out across 2026.

What this means for you

Signal 4

Seven state bills now bar AI from making final insurance coverage decisions. Federal law does not.

Seven bills across Alabama, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, and Massachusetts are moving to require human review of AI-assisted insurance denials and bar AI from making final coverage determinations. This is a different posture than the earlier wave of state disclosure laws. Those were about telling the patient. These are about holding back the AI. Roughly 200 state AI bills are being tracked in 2026, and much of the healthcare-relevant legislation took effect January 1 and applies regardless of federal guidance. ### The pattern this week Federal oversight is loosening, federal liability rules are being rewritten, European oversight arrives in August, and state-level restrictions are escalating from disclosure to outright prohibition. Underneath all of it, the best AI models in the world still fail the reasoning task most of the time when the information is incomplete. Which is, in a real practice, most of the time. This is not four separate stories. It is one story. The regulatory center of gravity is moving off the device and onto the practitioner. The question regulators, insurers, and courts will be asking through the rest of 2026 and into 2027 is not whether the tool was approved. It is whether the practitioner ran a documented process around it. ### One step this week Pick one AI-assisted tool in your practice. Write three sentences on how it is supervised: what the clinician reviews before a decision ships, what gets documented, and what the patient is told. If you cannot write those three sentences in under five minutes, you do not yet have a governance process for that tool. That is the starting line, not the finish line.
